There's a familiar pressure when a client asks "how long will this take?" The honest answer might be twelve weeks, but six is the number that wins the work. It's tempting — I've felt the pull.

I try not to give in to it, and not because I'm above it. I've just watched what the short number does: you win the deal, then spend the next three months managing disappointment instead of building. Nobody enjoys that stretch, least of all the client.

## The date I actually believe

When I quote a timeline, I aim for the date I genuinely think we'll hit, with some room for the things that usually go sideways. Sometimes that makes my number bigger than the next quote, and sometimes I lose the work because of it. I've made peace with that trade.

A few habits keep me honest:

- Saying no to work I can't do well in the time we have.
- Flagging early when something turns out harder than it looked.
- Trying not to bill for my own uncertainty.

> An estimate is a quiet promise. If I wouldn't make the promise out loud, I try not to hide it inside a number.

## Buffer, don't inflate

There's a real difference between leaving room for known unknowns and padding to cover myself. The first is just planning. The second is charging someone for my nerves, and I'd rather not.

So I'm occasionally not the cheapest quote in the room. That's a trade I'm comfortable with — I'd rather under-promise and show up than win on a date I can't keep.
